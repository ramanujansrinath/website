Interactive Shape Morphs

Creating shape you can interact with for human psychophysics

For Secret Reasons™, I had to come up with a set of objects I could ‘interact’ with. (The central idea here keeps recurring in my day dreams and is taking the shape of something I want to study as a PI — more on that another time.) I started off with some random shapes as described elsewhere.

The two interesting manipulations I thought of at the time were both inspired by interacting with touchscreen devices — pinch-to-zoom and crop. One would use two finger to pinch-to-zoom to squish the object along an axis, and use one finger to move a part of the object closer or farther away from the object centre. These two interactions could become the basis of a psychophysical study — “modify this shape to resemble a shape you saw before” or something like that. I later added two more morphs to “blockify” or “break up” shapes. You could double-tap on the shape to break it up and join them back up by dragging your fingers across two shapes. You could draw a square around the shape with your finger to make it blocky and draw curves to make it curvy.

The scientific question and the details of how you control for differences in motor outputs, perceptual differences between shape features, etc. are all super interesting but beyond the scope of this post. As with a lot of these projects, this one was technically very satisfying to solve and may become a part of a different project later in life. There are several pieces of interesting shape nerd-ery under the hood — forming shapes from Voronoi tessellations, keeping track of cubic b-splines as they change across morphs, comparing shapes formed by rectilinear blocks vs curved contours, etc. Cool stuff!

Edit (Jan 2022): I find the “blockify” filter above fascinating. I wanted to see if it would work in 3D. The result was really satisfying.

(PS: Also, not only do some of my images bear striking resemblance to this illustration from “The quantitative study of shape and pattern perception”, this body of work is inspired in part from decades of careful shape analysis starting from Fred Attneave, David Marr, Harry Blum, J Anthony Deutsch and many more I am forgetting.)
